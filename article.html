Why Most Topic Research Methods Fail

Most writers do one of three things:

They copy what’s already popular

They guess what people want

They write what they personally find interesting

None of these work consistently.

Here’s why:

1. Popular topics are already covered by bigger websites with more authority. When you copy them, you’re always playing catch-up.

2. Guessing what people want leads to articles nobody searches for.

3. Writing only what interests you might feel good, but it doesn’t guarantee readers.

My method fixes all these problems.

The 7-Layer Research Method

Think of topic research as digging for treasure. Most people scratch the surface and find nothing.

You need to dig through seven layers to find gold.

Layer 1: Pain Point Mining

People read articles to solve problems. The bigger the problem, the more they want a solution.

Here’s how I find real pain points:

I hang out in Reddit communities related to my topic

I read comments, not just posts

I look for phrases like “I can’t figure out,” “I’m struggling with,” or “How do I”

I save these questions in a spreadsheet

Example: When researching productivity topics, I found dozens of comments like “I make to-do lists but never finish them” and “I start projects but can’t complete them.”

This became my article “Why Your Brain Sabotages Your To-Do List (And How to Fix It)” which got 37,000 views on Medium.

Layer 2: Competitive Gap Analysis

This step sounds fancy but it’s simple:

Find the top 10 articles on your topic

List what they cover

Look for what they all miss

I use a simple table with article titles on top and subtopics on the side.

I mark which articles cover which subtopics. Anything with few checkmarks is a gap.

Example: I noticed fitness articles about home workouts all focused on exercises but ignored how to stay motivated without a gym environment.

My article “The Psychological Tricks That Make Home Workouts Actually Stick” filled this gap and went viral during the pandemic.

Layer 3: Trend Timing

Topics have seasons. Knowing when interest peaks helps your article hit at the perfect time.

I use Google Trends but in a specific way:

Check the five-year view to spot yearly patterns

Look for topics trending upward

Plan to publish 2–3 weeks before peak interest

Example: I noticed searches for “how to sleep better” spike in January and August. I published sleep articles in mid-December and mid-July, which positioned them perfectly for the rush of searchers.

Layer 4: Comment Mining for Language

The words people use matter. I collect the exact phrases people use when talking about their problems.

I take screenshots of comments from:

YouTube videos on my topic

Amazon reviews of related products

Facebook groups

Twitter/X discussions

I’ve built a “language bank” of real phrases people use about different topics.

Example: For a money-saving article, I noticed people rarely said “I want to budget better” but often said, “I need to stop bleeding money.”

Using their language in my headlines and intros doubled my click rates.

Layer 5: Contrarian Angle Finding
For any popular belief, there’s a group of people who have found it doesn’t work for them.

To find contrarian angles:

List common advice on your topic

Search for “why [common advice] doesn’t work”

Read the stories of people who tried and failed

Example: Everyone says “wake up at 5 AM to be successful.” I found hundreds of night owls who tried this and crashed.

My article “Why Waking Up at 5 AM Destroyed My Productivity (And What Actually Worked)” resonated with this underserved audience.

Layer 6: Problem Stacking

Most articles solve one problem.

Viral articles solve multiple connected problems.

I list related problems and stack them in one article:

Main problem → Related problem → Deeper problem

Example: Instead of just writing “How to Meal Prep,” I wrote “How to Meal Prep When You Hate Cooking, Have No Time, and Get Bored Easily.”

This addressed three stacked problems and outperformed basic meal prep articles by 5x.

Layer 7: Headline Testing

Before writing a single word of the article, I test 5–10 headline ideas:

I post headline options in relevant Facebook groups asking which they’d click

I create simple Twitter/X polls

I ask 3–5 friends which headline makes them most curious

I only write articles with headlines that get strong positive reactions.

Example: For a productivity article, I tested:

“10 Productivity Hacks for Busy People”

“Why Your Productivity System Is Making You Less Productive”

“The Post-It Note Method That Doubled My Daily Output”

The third one got 3x more interest in testing and became my most shared article that month.
